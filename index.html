Pneumocystis Pneumonia (PCP) - Summary of Information

Summary

Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PCP) is an opportunistic fungal lung infection that primarily affects immunocompromised individuals. It is strongly associated with HIV/AIDS but can also occur in patients receiving immunosuppressive therapy, those with malignancies, or individuals with primary immunodeficiency disorders.

Clinical Features – Observable Signs and Symptoms of Disease

  • Progressive dyspnea (shortness of breath)
  • Nonproductive cough – a dry cough that does not produce mucus
  • Fever
  • Hypoxia (low levels of oxygen in body tissues) disproportionate to clinical findings

Symptoms develop gradually over days to weeks.

Diagnosis

Laboratory markers – biochemical or molecular indicators measured using bodily fluids:

  • Elevated β-D-glucan – a fungal cell wall component and polysaccharide
  • Increased lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) – an enzyme found in almost all body tissues

Imaging (Chest X-ray or CT scan):

  • Diffuse bilateral infiltrates (abnormal opacities/lumps seen in both lungs) with a ground-glass appearance

Definitive diagnosis can be made by detecting P. jirovecii in induced sputum, bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL), or lung biopsy via:

  • Giemsa, Methenamine silver stain, or Immunofluorescence staining – specialized techniques used to identify organisms
  • PCR testing (Polymerase Chain Reaction) – a highly sensitive and specific molecular technique

Treatment

First-line therapy: Trimethoprim/Sulfamethoxazole (TMP/SMX) (high dose)

Adjunctive therapy: Glucocorticoids (if PaO₂ < 70 mmHg or A-a gradient > 35 mmHg)

Prophylaxis: Treatment taken to prevent disease is indicated for patients with:

  • HIV and CD4 < 200 cells/µL
  • Other immunocompromised states (e.g., prolonged corticosteroid use)

Prophylactic regimen: Low-dose TMP/SMX (preferred)

Additional Treatment Information

The most common treatment is trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole (TMP/SMX), also known as co-trimoxazole. It is given by mouth or through a vein for 3 weeks. TMP/SMX may cause side effects such as rash and fever. Alternative medications are available for patients who cannot take TMP/SMX.

Note: Unlike other fungal diseases, PCP is treated with an antibiotic (TMP/SMX), and antifungal medications do not work to treat it.
